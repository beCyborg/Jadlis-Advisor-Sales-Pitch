# Testing, Rollout, Recertification, and Extended Use
## Reference: Sales Pitch — April Dunford (2023)

---

## 1. Testing the Pitch

### Core Rule

Do not roll out a new pitch to the entire sales team at once. Run a controlled test with a single representative first.

### Selecting the Test Rep

| Criterion | Why It Matters |
|-----------|---------------|
| Respected by the rest of the team | Their endorsement carries weight during rollout; peers will trust their judgment |
| Ideally involved in storyboard creation | Already familiar with the logic and flow; shorter ramp-up time |
| Trained and comfortable before first live use | Do not expose real prospects to an unprepared rep |

Marketing and sales leadership must verify readiness before the rep pitches to prospects.

### Test Execution Steps

1. Train the test rep on the full deck, demo flow, and script.
2. Rep begins pitching to real, qualified prospects — not internal stand-ins.
3. Pitch creators (marketing, product marketing, sales leadership) listen to every call during the test period.
4. After each call, the team reviews:
   - Where did the prospect get confused or go quiet?
   - Where did the prospect show engagement or excitement?
   - What questions indicated misunderstanding?
5. Make targeted adjustments: wording, vocabulary, minor additions to clarify.
6. Do not make structural changes during the test. Structural rewrites invalidate the test data and restart the clock.

### Dual-Pass Validation Criteria

Both criteria must be met before the pitch is considered validated.

**Pass 1 — Rep Confidence**

The test rep believes the pitch no longer needs further refinement. They are satisfied with the overall flow. Minor wording details may still be in flux, but the structure is solid.

**Pass 2 — Rep Preference**

The test rep has actively chosen to use the new pitch because it is clearly better than the old one. This criterion accounts for comfort bias: reps default to what they already know. If the rep has tried the new pitch multiple times and still prefers the old one, the test has not passed.

Both passes must succeed. One alone is insufficient.

### If the Test Fails

Failure is rare but recoverable. Two root causes:

| Root Cause | Diagnosis | Corrective Action |
|------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Sales team was not involved enough in creation | Rep doesn't understand or believe the logic behind the pitch | Re-run the storyboard creation process with deeper sales involvement |
| Positioning inputs are weak | The differentiated value in the story doesn't hold up under prospect questioning | Return to positioning work; see Jadlis-Advisor-Positioning (`/jadlis-advisor-positioning:advisor`) |

Do not attempt to patch a fundamentally broken pitch with wording tweaks. Fix the root cause.

## 2. Rolling Out to the Sales Team

### Training Package Components

Deliver these four elements when rolling out to the full team:

1. **Video of the test rep doing the pitch** — not a screen recording of slides. The rep on camera demonstrating the flow gives other reps a concrete model to follow.
2. **Q&A session with the test rep** — peers asking a peer questions about the pitch. Credibility is higher than if sales leadership presents it.
3. **Practice pitches** — rep-to-rep or rep-to-manager. Reps must say the words out loud before pitching to prospects.
4. **Certification** — each rep pitches to sales leadership and receives sign-off before engaging real prospects.

### Why Reps Resist New Pitches

Resistance is normal and expected. The existing pitch feels natural because reps have practiced it hundreds of times. A new pitch feels awkward regardless of how much better it is. This is not a signal of failure.

The test rep's endorsement is the most effective counter to resistance. Peer credibility ("this rep tried it, it worked, they prefer it") overrides leadership mandates.

**Common objection**: "I don't want to sound like a robot."

Response: The script and deck are a foundation, not a cage. Experienced reps can improvise, add anecdotes, and express personality. What must not change:

- The flow: Setup phase before Follow-Through phase
- The focus on differentiated value (not feature walkthrough)
- The discovery conversation structure

The ordering and logic of the pitch are fixed. The tone and delivery are the rep's own.

## 3. Recertification and Pitch Maintenance

### Problem: Pitch Drift

Left unmanaged, pitches deteriorate:

- Individual reps make small unilateral changes that seem harmless in isolation
- New reps learn from existing reps rather than from canonical materials, compounding errors
- Market conditions shift, but the pitch does not

After 12 months without maintenance, many sales teams are running materially different pitches from the one originally designed.

### Pitch Ownership

Assign a named owner — typically someone in product marketing. Responsibilities:

- Maintain canonical documentation of the positioning, pitch structure, and approved assets
- Schedule and run regular cross-functional check-in meetings (sales, marketing, product)
- Initiate pitch updates when positioning inputs change

Without a designated owner, no one is accountable for drift.

### Recertification Cadence

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| New rep hired | Train on canonical pitch → certify before first prospect call |
| Existing rep (periodic) | Pitch to sales exec → receive feedback → confirm alignment |
| Significant product update | Reassess differentiators → update pitch → recertify all reps |
| New competitor enters market | Update Alternatives section → check if differentiators still hold → recertify |
| Positioning shift | Full pitch revision → re-run training and certification |

### What to Monitor

Watch for these drift signals:

- Reps skip the Setup phase and revert to a feature walkthrough
- Marketing and sales are describing the product differently to buyers
- New competitors appear that are not represented in the Alternatives list
- Product has new differentiators that are not in the pitch
- Former differentiators have been matched by competitors and are no longer distinctive
- Buyer questions have shifted to topics the current pitch does not address

### Positioning Review Cadence

Review positioning formally at least twice per year. Trigger an out-of-cycle review when:

- A significant competitor launches or shuts down
- The product roadmap changes the differentiation profile
- Buyer behavior or spending priorities shift visibly (e.g., economic conditions, regulatory changes)

When positioning changes, the sales pitch must change. When the sales pitch changes, downstream marketing content must align.

## 4. Using the Pitch Story Beyond Sales Calls

The narrative structure built for the sales pitch is reusable across channels. The same insight, alternatives framing, differentiation, and value logic can be adapted for:

### 4.1 Explainer Video (Homepage)

- Follows the storyboard structure in compressed form
- Communicates insight → differentiation → value → proof in approximately 2 minutes
- Ensures the first impression a buyer gets from the website matches what sales communicates in calls
- Production note: the video script can be derived directly from the storyboard

### 4.2 Buyer's Guide (Lead Generation Asset)

- Based on Step 1 (Insight): what should an informed buyer evaluate?
- Uses the Alternatives framing: organize the guide around categories of tools buyers consider, with commentary on when each fits
- Helps buyers understand which capabilities matter and why — from a knowledgeable, neutral-seeming perspective
- Conversion signal: someone downloading a buyer's guide is actively evaluating; treat as high-intent lead
- Format: categorized checklists with brief contextual commentary

### 4.3 Product Onboarding and Help Content

- For product-led growth products: the in-product experience should reinforce the differentiated value, not just teach feature mechanics
- Onboarding flows should surface differentiators early, not bury them in advanced settings
- Caution: the champion or economic buyer (who approved the purchase) may have different priorities than the end user (who uses the product daily). Do not assume the sales pitch positioning applies unchanged to end-user onboarding. Verify which positioning resonates with each audience.

### 4.4 Conference Talks

- The Setup phase — especially the Insight (Step 1) — is natural material for a conference talk
- A talk built around a compelling market insight positions the company as a thought leader without being a product pitch
- What works: sharing a unique, data-supported point of view on a market problem
- What does not work: presenting the full sales pitch on stage — that reads as a sales session

### 4.5 Research and White Papers

- Commission or conduct original research that explores the themes of your Insight
- Research results serve as Step 1 evidence in the pitch and can be cited in calls
- Repurpose findings into: white papers, blog posts, social content, press coverage
- The research topic should map directly to the buyer problem framed in the Setup phase

### 4.6 Visual Assets

Visuals derived from the pitch narrative:

| Asset Type | Content |
|------------|---------|
| Product suite diagram | Shows how components relate; useful when product has multiple modules |
| Company portfolio illustration | Shows product family context for multi-product companies |
| "How we fit into your stack" graphic | Maps product to buyer's existing tools and workflow |
| Workflow graphic | Illustrates the specific point in a buyer's process where the product delivers value |

These visuals can be embedded in decks, websites, and sales leave-behinds. They should reflect the differentiated value framing, not a neutral feature diagram.

### Alignment Principle

Building the sales pitch from positioning guarantees that the central narrative is consistent. Marketing content, sales conversations, onboarding, and conference talks all derive from the same source. When positioning changes, the pitch changes, and all downstream content updates from there.

Without this anchor, marketing and sales drift into telling different stories — a common failure mode in B2B companies.

## 5. Positioning Change Over Time

Positioning is not permanent. The following forces cause positioning to become outdated:

| Force | Effect on Positioning |
|-------|-----------------------|
| Product capability changes | New differentiators emerge; old ones become table stakes |
| Competitor product upgrades | Former differentiators get matched and neutralized |
| New competitors enter | Alternatives list is incomplete; differentiation claims may be undermined |
| Competitors exit | Alternatives list references dead products; comparison may need reframing |
| Buyer preference shifts | Formerly valued capabilities become less important; new priorities emerge |
| Economic or regulatory changes | Spending priorities shift; value framing needs adjustment |

When any of these occur:

1. Reassess positioning inputs (see Jadlis-Advisor-Positioning for the full cascade)
2. Revise the sales pitch narrative accordingly
3. Update all downstream assets: explainer video, buyer's guide, website copy, onboarding flows
4. Recertify sales team on the updated pitch

Treat positioning and the sales pitch as living documents with defined owners, not artifacts created once and archived.
