# Setup Phase — Steps 1–3

Source: *Sales Pitch* by April Dunford (2023)

The Setup Phase (Steps 1–3) precedes any product demonstration or feature discussion. Its function is to install a decision-making framework in the prospect's mind — one that makes your differentiated value the logical conclusion. By the end of Step 3, a prospect who agrees ("Right?") has already pre-selected your value before seeing your product.

---

## Step 1: Insight

### What Insight Is

Insight is your unique point of view on the market. It surfaces "the problem inside the problem" — something the prospect needs to understand in order to recognize why your differentiated value matters to them specifically.

Insight answers the question: **"What must the buyer know to see why our unique value is critical?"**

It is derived from your differentiated value, not from generic market observations. It points toward your value, not toward a problem that every competitor also claims to solve.

### What Insight Is NOT

| Incorrect | Why it fails |
|-----------|--------------|
| Generic market trend ("AI is accelerating", "data volumes are growing") | Any competitor can say this. It points to no specific solution |
| The problem your product solves | If every competitor solves the same problem, this is not an insight — it's category framing |
| Company vision or mission | Mission is about you. Insight is about what the buyer needs to understand |

**Test**: Could a direct competitor open their pitch with the same statement? If yes, the insight is not differentiated enough. Drill deeper until the answer is no.

### Derivation Algorithm

Execute this sequence to derive insight from positioning:

```
1. Identify your differentiated value
   (the capability or outcome only you can deliver to your target segment)

2. Ask: "What does the buyer need to understand to see why this value is
   critical for them — not just nice-to-have?"

3. The answer to step 2 is your candidate insight.

4. Run the competitor test:
   - Could any of your top 3 competitors open with this same statement?
   - If YES → the insight is too generic. Return to step 2, go deeper.
   - If NO → proceed.

5. Check directionality:
   - Does the insight point toward YOUR specific differentiated value?
   - If it points toward a general category problem → too broad. Refine.
```

### Format

- 1–2 slides maximum
- 1–2 minutes of spoken discussion
- Supporting data or research is acceptable if it reinforces the insight (not if it restates market size or generic trends)
- Can be delivered as a statement of fact — does not need to be framed as a revelation
- The key constraint: the insight must point toward your value, not toward a generic problem definition

### Worked Examples

**LevelJump** (sales enablement built on Salesforce):
- Generic version (too weak): "Efficiently training sales reps is a challenge."
- Insight: "Sales enablement should be measured with sales metrics — time to first deal, time to quota attainment — not just training completion rates."
- Why it works: Only LevelJump, built on Salesforce, can track enablement activities and connect them to actual sales outcomes. The insight points exclusively to that capability.

**Postman** (API platform):
- Generic version (too weak): "APIs are important."
- Insight: "APIs are becoming critical building blocks of digital products, but most companies develop them as an afterthought using siloed, unconnected tools — putting customer experience and revenue at risk."
- Why it works: Points directly to the need for an integrated, full-lifecycle API platform.

**Funnel** (marketing data hub):
- Generic version (too weak): "Marketers need data."
- Insight: "Great digital marketing depends entirely on data, but collecting it across channels, normalizing it, and producing usable metrics is difficult, time-consuming, and error-prone when done manually."
- Why it works: Points to the need for an automated, centralized marketing data hub.

**Gearset** (Salesforce DevOps):
- Generic version (too weak): "DevOps practices improve software delivery."
- Insight: "The same factors that produce DevOps success in general software engineering — version control, CI/CD, automated testing — apply equally to Salesforce releases."
- Why it works: Points to a Salesforce DevOps tool built on standard DevOps toolchain integrations, not a proprietary workaround.

## Step 2: Alternatives

### Core Principle

Prospects do not compare your product against a list of competing products. They compare **approaches to solving a problem**. Grouping competitors into approaches is how buyers actually think, and how the sales conversation must be structured.

You compete with approaches. Individual company names are secondary.

### Clustering Process

```
1. List ALL alternatives the prospect might consider:
   - Status quo: how they solve this today without a specialized tool
     (Excel, manual process, "hire an intern", existing monolith, do nothing)
   - Direct competitors that appear on actual prospect shortlists
   - Adjacent tools they might repurpose

   EXCLUDE: "horizon" competitors the prospect hasn't encountered.
   Do not introduce unknown competitors. Name only what the prospect
   has almost certainly already considered.

2. Group listed alternatives into 2–4 clusters based on approach
   (how each group solves the problem, not by vendor category)

3. For each cluster, document:
   - Cluster label (short descriptive name, not brand names)
   - What this approach involves
   - Pros: what it does well for this target customer
   - Cons: where it falls short — especially gaps your solution fills

4. Format as a comparison table (see below)
```

### Comparison Table Format

| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| [Cluster 1 label] | [What works] | [Where it fails] |
| [Cluster 2 label] | [What works] | [Where it fails] |
| [Cluster 3 label] | [What works] | [Where it fails] |

The cons column is load-bearing. Those gaps are what Step 3 (Perfect World) will address.

### Worked Examples

**Project management tool for marketing agencies**:

| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| Manual (Excel, docs) | Free, no learning curve | Cannot manage cross-project resource allocation |
| Easy all-purpose tools (Trello, Basecamp) | Low cost, easy adoption | Lack multi-project resource planning for agencies |
| Enterprise tools (Jira) | Full-featured | Expensive, complex, overkill for agency scale |

**Help Scout** (customer support platform):

| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| Shared email inbox | Simple, familiar | Missing advanced features; no scalability |
| Traditional help desk | Feature-rich | Designed for cost reduction, not customer experience |

**LevelJump** (sales enablement):

| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| Shared drive | No cost, immediate | Cannot track what reps consume or learn |
| LMS (generic) | Tracks completion | Cannot measure impact on sales outcomes |

**Funnel** (marketing data hub):

| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| Connectors + spreadsheets | Simple, low cost | Siloed; manual normalization; error-prone |
| IT-managed data stack | Data accessible | Marketing has no control; IT backlog dependency |
| Marketing analytics platforms | Broad coverage | Lack depth; vendor lock-in on analysis layer |

### Discovery Integration

Step 2 is a dialogue, not a lecture. The rep teaches the framework while gathering information. Weave questions throughout:

```
"How are you handling [problem] today?"
"How is that working out for you?"
"What other solutions are you evaluating?"
"Have you found that [alternative approach] meets your needs
  for [specific capability]?"
```

The rep learns which alternatives the prospect has actually considered and which pain points with those alternatives are most acute. Both parties deepen understanding simultaneously. Do not deliver the alternatives table as a monologue.

### Naming Competitors

Default rule: **do not name specific companies** in the alternatives table.

Exceptions:
- The prospect has almost certainly already seen this competitor on their shortlist
- Not naming them would seem evasive or incomplete

Introducing a competitor the prospect hasn't encountered creates unnecessary objection surface and wastes their evaluation bandwidth. Cluster by approach instead.

## Step 3: Perfect World + "Right?" Gate

### Purpose

Step 3 concludes the Setup phase. It translates the Insight and Alternatives discussion into a set of purchase criteria that map precisely to your differentiated value — stated in the prospect's language, not yours.

When the prospect agrees ("Right?"), they have pre-selected your value before the product demonstration begins. The Follow-Through (product demo and proof) becomes confirmation, not persuasion.

### Construction Process

```
1. Draw the criteria from two sources:
   - Insight (Step 1): what capability or understanding your insight pointed toward
   - Alternatives cons column (Step 2): the gaps in each approach the prospect acknowledged

2. Construct 2–4 criteria, each structured as:
   "[upside of approach X] without [downside of approach X]"
   or
   "[capability the insight showed was critical]"

3. Each criterion must:
   - Reflect something the prospect acknowledged as a real need
   - Map to a capability you actually provide
   - NOT be something every competitor equally provides

4. Order criteria to flow naturally from the prior discussion.
   Do not introduce criteria with no prior setup.

5. Deliver as a single summary statement with the "Right?" close.
```

### Script Template

```
"Based on what we've discussed, in a perfect world you'd have a solution that:

- [Upside of Approach 1] without [Downside of Approach 1]
- [Upside of Approach 2] without [Downside of Approach 2]
- [Criterion derived from insight]

Right?"
```

### Worked Examples

**Project management for marketing agencies**:
```
"In a perfect world you'd have something that's as easy to use as Trello,
designed specifically for multi-project resource management,
without the cost or complexity of enterprise tools like Jira.
Right?"
```

**Help Scout**:
```
"Ideally you'd have something as easy to use as a shared inbox,
with the advanced features you won't outgrow as you scale,
and highly focused on delivering a great customer experience —
not just cost reduction.
Right?"
```

**Postman**:
```
"In a perfect world you'd have integrated tools that span the full
API lifecycle, easy team collaboration built in, and everything your
API consumers need in one place.
Right?"
```

**Funnel**:
```
"What you'd want is something that pulls data from any source automatically,
stores it in a central hub that marketing owns and controls,
auto-prepares it for analysis, and lets you share it anywhere.
Right?"
```

### "Right?" Branching Logic

The prospect's response determines the next action. Execute the correct branch:

| Response type | Signal | Action |
|---------------|--------|--------|
| **YES** — "Yes, exactly" / "That's right" / strong agreement | Prospect endorses criteria that map to your unique value. They have pre-selected you. | Proceed immediately to Follow-Through (product demonstration). Do not re-litigate the criteria. |
| **PARTIAL** — "Mostly, but..." / "We also need X" / "Not sure about Y" | Some criteria don't resonate, or the prospect requires criteria you didn't include | Ask which criteria feel off. Probe: "What's missing for you?" Revisit the Alternatives table if needed. Adjust the Perfect World list. Re-ask "Right?" once revised. |
| **NO** — "Not really" / "That's not quite what we're looking for" | Prospect does not value the differentiators you uniquely provide | Disqualification signal. Options: (a) probe whether there is a different value theme that does resonate and restructure accordingly, (b) gracefully exit the conversation. Do not proceed to demo. |

### Critical Mechanism

The "Right?" gate is a pre-commitment mechanism. When the prospect says YES:

- They have agreed to a definition of the ideal solution
- That definition was constructed from YOUR differentiated capabilities
- The product demonstration will show that you satisfy those criteria
- Objections during demo are reduced because the criteria were established by the prospect, not the vendor

This is the structural pivot of the entire pitch. The Setup phase (Steps 1–3) exists to make this moment possible. If "Right?" is skipped or glossed over, the demo reverts to a feature-by-feature presentation with no pre-established evaluative frame.

### Timing and Format

- The Perfect World statement: 1–2 minutes to deliver
- Slide: optional. Sometimes a bullet-point list slide, sometimes delivered verbally with no visual
- Do not turn it into a lengthy discussion on its own. It is a summary of what was already discussed, not new information
- The new element is only the synthesis and the explicit "Right?" close

---

## Setup Phase Summary

| Step | Duration | Output | Gate |
|------|----------|--------|------|
| 1. Insight | 1–2 min | Prospect understands WHY your differentiated value is critical | None — informational |
| 2. Alternatives | 3–7 min (dialogue) | Prospect has mapped their current situation to approach gaps | Discovery questions answered |
| 3. Perfect World | 1–2 min | Criteria list that maps to your value | "Right?" — YES to proceed |

The Setup phase creates the evaluative frame. Everything in Follow-Through (demo, proof, case studies) is measured against that frame. Run all three steps before showing any product capability.
