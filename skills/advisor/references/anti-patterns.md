# Anti-Patterns Reference — Sales Pitch (April Dunford, 2023)

## Purpose

Diagnostic reference for the 8-step storyboard. Use at any stage when a user describes or shares a pitch, demo, or sales conversation. Match symptoms to named anti-patterns. Address only those that apply. Do not enumerate all 12 unprompted.

---

## The 12 Named Anti-Patterns

### 1. Feature Walkthrough

**Stage**: Step 5 (Value Demo)

**What it is**: Demo organized around product navigation and menu structure rather than value themes. Every feature gets shown regardless of differentiation. Setup/admin/login flows are included. No value framing precedes or follows feature demonstrations.

**Diagnostic signal**:
- Demo sequence mirrors the product's left-nav or top-menu
- Rep shows login, account creation, or admin panels
- Non-differentiating features receive equal time as differentiating ones
- After the demo, prospect has seen a lot but can't articulate why this product vs. alternatives

**Why it fails**: The prospect drowns in capabilities with no frame to evaluate them. The demo answers "what does it do?" but never answers "why should I care, given my alternatives?" A feature-dense demo leaves prospects feeling overwhelmed rather than clear. The cognitive load is on the buyer to figure out which features matter — that work should be the rep's.

**What to do instead**: Identify maximum 3 value themes derived from your differentiated capabilities. Structure the demo around those themes, not around the product's navigation. For each feature shown: state the value theme first, demonstrate the feature, confirm the value landed. Pattern: "We deliver [Value A] — here's how." Never show a feature without connecting it to a value theme.

### 2. Generic Insight / Insight Not Unique

**Stage**: Step 1 (Insight)

**What it is**: Opening the pitch with a market trend, problem statement, or observation that every competitor in the space could also open with.

**Diagnostic signal**:
- Replace the company name with any competitor — the insight still holds
- Examples of generic insights: "Data volumes are growing", "AI is changing every industry", "Companies need to digitally transform", "Remote work is the new normal"
- The insight does not point toward anything specifically valuable about this company's product

**Why it fails**: The insight is supposed to establish credibility and orient the buyer toward the differentiated value that follows. A generic insight does neither. It sets up a pitch that any competitor could give. The buyer gets no signal that this rep or product understands the market in a way that's different or more sophisticated.

**What to do instead**: Reverse-engineer the insight from the differentiated value. Ask: "What must a buyer believe or understand about the market in order to see why OUR specific differentiated value matters?" The insight should be something your competitors would not lead with — either because they don't have the same differentiation, or because saying it would expose their weaknesses. If a competitor could repeat your insight verbatim, it is not the right insight.

### 3. Vision Narrative

**Stage**: General (most common in Step 1, but infects the entire pitch)

**What it is**: Using an investor pitch narrative structure — "old world vs. new world", "the old way was broken, the new way is us" — as the frame for a sales pitch.

**Diagnostic signal**:
- Pitch describes a future state, a market disruption, or a company mission
- Language includes: "We're disrupting X", "The future of Y looks like Z", "Next-generation [category]", "We're building the [X] of the future"
- Heavy focus on what will be true, not what is true today
- Pitch sounds like something you'd hear at a startup investor conference

**Why it fails**: Investor pitches and sales pitches serve opposite purposes. An investor pitch needs to generate excitement about a future bet. A sales pitch needs to help a buyer make a confident decision about what to purchase *today*, given the alternatives available today. A vision narrative gives buyers reasons to delay: "Come back when this exists." In B2B, "new" is not automatically valuable — it can signal untested, unproven, risky. The vision narrative skips the entire question of competitive alternatives, which is the central question of a sales pitch.

**What to do instead**: Anchor the pitch in the present. The central question the pitch must answer is: "Why should I buy from you, compared to the other options I could choose right now?" Save the vision narrative for investor decks, all-hands presentations, and recruiting materials.

### 4. Problem/Solution Trap

**Stage**: Steps 1–3

**What it is**: Structuring the pitch as: here is a problem → we are the solution. The problem is defined at a level of generality that every competitor in the space also addresses.

**Diagnostic signal**:
- Pitch includes a "problem slide" listing pain points that any competitor could also claim to solve
- Example: "Finance teams waste time on manual reconciliation" — if every accounting software company could say this, it is the trap
- No discussion of alternative approaches to the problem
- The pitch jumps from problem directly to product features

**Why it fails**: Stating a generic problem and claiming to be "the solution" provides no basis for differentiation. Every competitor also solves "the problem." The structure inherently skips the alternatives landscape — which is where differentiated value lives. After the problem statement, there is nowhere to go but a feature walkthrough, because the pitch hasn't established what unique value it needs to prove.

**What to do instead**: Replace the problem/solution structure with the Setup phase of the storyboard. Start with a unique Insight (Step 1), discuss the alternative Approaches buyers consider (Step 2 — this is where differentiation becomes visible), then define Perfect World criteria (Step 3) that map explicitly to your unique capabilities. The problem is implicit in this structure; the alternatives and criteria make the differentiation explicit.

### 5. Hero's Journey Trap

**Stage**: General

**What it is**: Applying the StoryBrand / hero's journey framework to a B2B sales pitch. The customer is positioned as the hero, the company as the guide, and the pitch culminates in a "plan" (typically: buy our software → follow these steps → achieve transformation).

**Diagnostic signal**:
- Pitch explicitly positions the customer as the hero navigating a challenge
- Company is framed as the wise guide or mentor figure
- The "plan" in the pitch describes implementation steps or onboarding sequences
- Focus is on customer transformation, not on how to choose between competing alternatives

**Why it fails**: The hero's journey / StoryBrand structure is optimized for a different problem: it helps a buyer take action once they have already decided they need a solution. It does not help a buyer understand why to choose this solution over alternatives. The "plan" in a hero's journey pitch is about execution. The missing piece is the entire question of competitive choice — how does a buyer confidently decide between you and the alternatives? That question is absent from the structure. The guide role is correct, but the guide must help the buyer make a confident CHOICE, not just start a journey.

**What to do instead**: Retain the guide framing — it is correct positioning for a sales rep. Replace the hero's journey arc with the 8-step storyboard, which includes the alternatives landscape (Step 2), the Perfect World criteria (Step 3), the Value Demo (Step 5), and the Proof (Step 6). These are the elements that enable a buying decision, not just a commitment to act.

### 6. Discovery Void

**Stage**: Step 2 (Alternatives)

**What it is**: Presenting information about the alternatives landscape as a lecture, with the rep talking and the prospect listening. No discovery questions are asked. The rep has a fully-built competitive analysis slide and reads through it.

**Diagnostic signal**:
- Prospect is passive during the Alternatives discussion
- Rep presents pros/cons of each alternative approach without asking the prospect about their current setup
- No questions like "How are you handling this today?" or "What have you looked at?"
- Prospect's actual situation is unknown after the Alternatives step

**Why it fails**: The Alternatives discussion is supposed to serve two purposes simultaneously: teach the prospect about the landscape (rep-to-buyer) and learn about the prospect's actual situation (buyer-to-rep). A one-way lecture accomplishes only the first. The rep loses the opportunity to understand which alternative the prospect is actually comparing against, what they've already tried, and whether the market picture being painted matches the prospect's reality.

**What to do instead**: Weave discovery questions throughout the Alternatives discussion. For each alternative approach, after describing it, ask: "Is this how you're currently handling it?" or "Have you evaluated anything in this category?" The goal is a two-way conversation where the rep teaches market context and the prospect reveals their situation. Both parties learn.

### 7. Discovery as Interrogation

**Stage**: Step 2 (Alternatives)

**What it is**: Opposite problem from Discovery Void. The rep asks a long sequence of qualification questions — budget, timeline, requirements, pain points — without providing any market context or insight in return.

**Diagnostic signal**:
- Meeting consists primarily of questions from the rep
- Questions are qualification-focused: "What's your budget?", "What's your timeline?", "What are your requirements?", "What are your biggest pain points?"
- Prospect is giving information but receiving none
- Rep appears to have no more knowledge of the market than the prospect

**Why it fails**: Buyers frequently don't know their own requirements at the start of a sales process — especially for categories they haven't bought before. Asking a buyer to articulate requirements they haven't formed yet produces low-quality answers and a frustrating experience. A rep who only asks questions signals that they have nothing to teach. The value of talking to a sales rep — beyond product demos — should be market insight. Discovery without teaching squanders this.

**What to do instead**: Structure discovery as a reciprocal conversation. The rep provides context (here are the approaches companies typically use), then asks (where does your current setup fit?). Each question is preceded or followed by useful market insight. The prospect gets smarter from the conversation; the rep gets qualified.

### 8. Setup Overrun

**Stage**: Steps 1–3

**What it is**: The Setup phase of the pitch (Steps 1–3: Insight, Alternatives, Perfect World) runs so long that insufficient time remains for the Value Demo (Step 5).

**Diagnostic signal**:
- 60-minute call with 20+ minutes spent on Setup
- Detailed competitive analysis slides with extensive feature comparison grids
- Long market education sequence before any product is shown
- Value Demo is rushed, skipped, or deferred to a follow-up meeting

**Why it fails**: The Value Demo (Step 5) is where the pitch proves its differentiated value. Setup is context — it frames the Demo so the buyer knows what to look for and why it matters. Context without proof does not close deals. If Setup consumes the available time, the pitch has successfully framed a value the buyer never got to see demonstrated. The Setup is the setup for the Demo; it is not the goal.

**What to do instead**: Time-box Setup explicitly. In a 60-minute meeting: Setup should be 5–10 minutes maximum, leaving 30–40 minutes for Value Demo and Proof. In a 30-minute meeting: Setup can be 90 seconds to 3 minutes. The Insight, Alternatives overview, and Perfect World summary can each be done in under 2 minutes when well-rehearsed. If the Setup requires more time to make sense, the content is too complex — simplify.

### 9. Undifferentiated Value Themes

**Stage**: Step 5 (Value Demo)

**What it is**: Value themes used to organize the demo that any competitor could claim with equal validity.

**Diagnostic signal**:
- Value themes include: "We save time", "We increase efficiency", "We improve productivity", "We reduce costs", "We make it easier"
- Competitor websites use the same language
- The value themes do not reference anything specific about how the product works or what makes it unique

**Why it fails**: If every competitor can claim the same value, the prospect has no basis for choosing one over another. The demo confirms that the product does what it says it does, but fails to demonstrate why this product over alternatives. Undifferentiated value themes are usually a symptom of weak positioning, not weak pitching — the pitch can only be as differentiated as the positioning it is built on.

**What to do instead**: Trace each value theme back to a differentiated capability — something the product does that alternatives cannot, or does significantly better. If this trace fails (the value is real but not differentiated), the positioning needs to be reworked before the pitch can be fixed. See `/jadlis-advisor-positioning:advisor` for the positioning process. Value themes must be specific enough that stating them immediately raises the question "how?" — and the demo answers it.

### 10. Proof Mismatch

**Stage**: Step 6 (Proof)

**What it is**: Using the same proof materials — case studies, testimonials, ROI figures — for every prospect regardless of their industry, company type, or stakeholder role.

**Diagnostic signal**:
- Single case study used across all verticals
- A banking customer's success story shown to a manufacturing prospect
- VP Engineering testimonial shown to a CFO
- "We only have one case study right now" used as the default proof for all pitches

**Why it fails**: Proof works by allowing the prospect to see themselves in the success story. A prospect from a different industry, company size, or function cannot easily map another company's experience to their own situation. The natural response is: "That's interesting, but our situation is different." Mismatched proof reduces credibility rather than building it, because it signals the rep doesn't know who they're talking to.

**What to do instead**: Build a proof library organized across two dimensions: (1) company type / industry, and (2) stakeholder role. Select proof based on the specific prospect in the meeting. A manufacturing CFO should see: a manufacturing company's proof AND a CFO-relevant success metric. When the library is thin, acknowledge the match explicitly: "This is from a different industry, but the challenge they faced was identical to what you described — here's why it's relevant."

### 11. Complicated Ask

**Stage**: Step 8 (Ask)

**What it is**: The closing ask is vague, requires too much commitment for the stage of the relationship, or involves multiple complex steps before the next action.

**Diagnostic signal**:
- Ask is: "Let us know if you have any questions" (too vague — no clear next step)
- Ask is: "We'd like to propose a 3-year enterprise agreement" (too high commitment for early-stage conversation)
- Next steps require VP sign-off, procurement review, or legal review before a second meeting can happen
- Prospect says "I'll think about it" and the meeting ends without a defined next action

**Why it fails**: When buyers face a decision, the path of least resistance is "no decision" — not "no," but "not yet." A complicated, vague, or high-commitment ask increases friction at the exact moment when the pitch should be reducing it. The Ask should make it easier for the prospect to say yes than to default to inaction.

**What to do instead**: Make the ask specific, low-commitment, and concrete. The ask should match the appropriate next step for the stage of the sales process. Early-stage example: "I recommend a 2-week proof of concept with 3 people from your team. We handle setup. Can we schedule a kickoff for next week?" The ask names the commitment (2 weeks, 3 people), explains who does the work (we do), and proposes a concrete next action (kickoff call).

### 12. Pitch Drift / Rollout Amnesia

**Stage**: Post-launch

**What it is**: After the pitch is built and trained, no ongoing maintenance is done. The pitch degrades over time as reps adapt it informally, new reps learn incorrect versions, and the market or product evolves without the pitch keeping pace.

**Diagnostic signal**:
- Different reps give meaningfully different pitches for the same product
- Reps hired after the initial pitch rollout learned the pitch informally from teammates rather than through structured training
- The pitch hasn't been updated despite significant product or positioning changes in the past 6–12 months
- No one can identify who is responsible for maintaining the pitch
- Reps have reverted to feature walkthroughs in their demos

**Why it fails**: A pitch is a living artifact. Positioning shifts, competitors evolve, products add capabilities, and markets change. A pitch that was accurate at launch will drift toward inaccuracy without active maintenance. More immediately, a pitch that is trained once and never reinforced will be modified by individual reps based on their personal experience, gradually reverting toward whatever default behavior existed before the pitch was built — typically, a feature walkthrough.

**What to do instead**: Assign a pitch owner (usually product marketing). Define a recertification cadence — at minimum, annually; ideally, at every significant positioning or product change. New reps must complete structured pitch training (observation, video review, Q&A, practice with feedback, certification) before pitching prospects. Maintain a video library of certified pitch examples for reference. Treat the pitch as a maintained artifact, not a completed project.

## Diagnosis Protocol

When a user describes a pitch, demo, sales meeting, or sales process:

1. Identify which signals are present in what they describe.
2. Name each matching anti-pattern explicitly.
3. State briefly why it fails in their specific context (not the generic definition).
4. State the corrective action.
5. If multiple anti-patterns co-occur, address them in stage order — earliest stage first (e.g., fix the Insight before fixing the Demo).

If Undifferentiated Value Themes is present, check whether the root cause is positioning. If yes, surface the `/jadlis-advisor-positioning:advisor` skill — the pitch cannot be fixed until the positioning is fixed.

If Pitch Drift is present, address the structural/organizational recommendations (ownership, recertification) — this is a process failure, not a content failure.

Do not enumerate anti-patterns that are not present in the user's situation.
