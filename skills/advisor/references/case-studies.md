# Case Studies: Worked Storyboard Examples

Five complete storyboard walkthroughs from the Sales Pitch methodology. Each shows how positioning inputs map to storyboard steps. Use these when a user's situation resembles a known pattern — apply the structural logic, not the surface details.

---

## 1. Help Scout

**Company**: Customer service platform, launched 2011. Competes against Zendesk and similar help desk tools. Built on a different philosophy: support as revenue driver and brand-builder, not cost center. Target customers are modern online businesses where the customer relationship is the product.

**Positioning inputs**:
- Competitive alternatives: shared email inbox; traditional help desk software (Zendesk, etc.)
- Unique capabilities: designed around customer experience, not ticket deflection; easy-to-use inbox metaphor with advanced features underneath
- Differentiated value: drives customer loyalty and referrals; doesn't make customers feel like a ticket number
- Best-fit customers: digital-first companies that view support as growth lever
- Market category: customer service software

### Step 1 — Insight

**Insight used**: "Customer service is an effective way to build brand loyalty and drive growth for digital brands. Great service decreases acquisition costs and drives referrals."

Why this insight works: it directly contradicts the framing that traditional help desks are built on (minimize cost, deflect tickets). The insight names a different goal — growth — and positions support as a strategic function. Any company that agrees with this framing will immediately feel the tension with traditional help desk tools, which are optimized for the opposite.

The insight is the setup move. It does not mention Help Scout. It establishes shared belief about what support *should* accomplish.

### Step 2 — Alternatives

Two approach clusters:

**Shared email inbox** (e.g., Gmail with multiple logins):
- Pros: easy to use, inbox metaphor everyone knows
- Cons: can't scale, no visibility into workload, no metrics, breaks as team grows

**Traditional help desk** (Zendesk, Freshdesk, etc.):
- Pros: built to scale, advanced routing and reporting features
- Cons: designed for cost reduction, not customer experience; hard to use; makes customers feel like ticket numbers; optimized for deflection

Both alternatives fail at something the insight said matters. Shared inbox fails on scale. Traditional help desk fails on customer experience — the thing the insight established as the goal.

### Step 3 — Perfect World

"Easy to use as a shared inbox AND advanced features to scale AND focused on delivering a great customer experience. Right?"

The "Right?" confirmation gates the demo. If the buyer agrees, they have implicitly accepted that neither existing alternative is good enough. Help Scout is the only logical conclusion.

### Steps 4-5 — Introduction and Value Demo

Transition: "That's exactly what we built."

Demo organized by three value themes (not feature categories):

1. **Easy-to-use inbox experience** — shows the inbox metaphor, no ticket numbers visible to customers
2. **Advanced support features you won't outgrow** — shows routing, reporting, workload visibility
3. **Delivering great customer experience** — shows customer-facing elements (no ticket numbers, customers reach out through any channel they choose)

Each theme maps directly to one leg of the Perfect World criteria.

### Steps 6-8 — Proof, Objections, Ask

**Proof**: customer stories from digital brands where support drove measurable growth (referrals, repeat purchase). Metrics tied to business outcomes, not ticket resolution time.

**Objections**: common objection is "we already use Zendesk." Counter-proof focuses on migration simplicity and contrast in customer satisfaction scores post-switch.

**Ask**: standard SaaS trial or demo-to-deal flow.

### Key Lesson

The storyboard makes the traditional help desk the wrong answer *before* Help Scout appears. The buyer eliminates the competitor through their own reasoning, not through a competitive slide. When the insight is strong and the alternatives are honestly described, the conclusion feels inevitable rather than sold.

## 2. LevelJump (acquired by Salesforce)

**Company**: Sales enablement platform. Helps salespeople learn to sell. Built natively on Salesforce, which allows it to connect training activity data directly to CRM sales performance data — a capability no standalone LMS can replicate.

**Positioning inputs**:
- Competitive alternatives: shared drives; Learning Management Systems (LMS)
- Unique capabilities: built on Salesforce, connects training completion to deal outcomes
- Differentiated value: measure enablement impact with actual sales metrics (time to first deal, time to quota)
- Best-fit customers: revenue operations and enablement teams who need to justify ROI to executives
- Market category: sales enablement

### Step 1 — Insight

**Insight used**: "Sales enablement should be measured with sales metrics — time to first deal, time to make quota — not just training completion. Every day reps aren't making quota costs money."

Why this insight works: it exposes a measurement gap that the entire category is blind to. LMS tools measure completion rates and quiz scores. But those metrics don't answer the question any VP of Sales actually cares about: are my reps ramping faster? The insight names the right metric. No LMS vendor would say this because no LMS can deliver it — they lack the CRM integration.

This is the pattern: derive the insight from your differentiated capability. The insight should be something only you can say because only you can back it up.

### Step 2 — Alternatives

Two approach clusters:

**Shared drive** (Google Drive, SharePoint):
- Pros: simple, no training required, everyone already has access
- Cons: can't track usage, can't measure impact, no structure, content goes stale

**LMS** (Learning Management System):
- Pros: tracks completion, organizes content by course, generates training reports
- Cons: disconnected from sales data, measures learning activity not sales outcomes, can't tell you if training moved quota

Both alternatives produce outputs (content access, completion rates) that cannot answer the actual business question established by the insight.

### Step 3 — Perfect World

"Measure the impact of enablement programs with actual sales metrics. Right?"

Shorter than Help Scout's Perfect World because the gap is simpler. The insight already planted the metric (sales outcomes). The Perfect World just restates it as the criterion.

### Steps 4-5 — Introduction and Value Demo

Transition: "That's what LevelJump is built to do."

Demo organized by three value themes:

1. **Track enablement impact on sales metrics** — shows the Salesforce integration, side-by-side view of training completion and deal progression
2. **Improve rep ramp time** — shows dashboards tracking time to first deal, segmented by training path taken
3. **Prove enablement team's value to executives** — shows executive reporting layer: ROI in sales terms, not learning terms

The third theme is strategically important. Enablement teams are chronically undervalued because they can't show impact in the language executives use. LevelJump gives them the evidence to secure budget.

### Steps 6-8 — Proof, Objections, Ask

**Proof**: case studies showing reduction in time-to-quota, with before/after metrics from CRM. The data is credible because it comes from Salesforce, which the buyer already trusts.

**Objections**: "We already have an LMS." Counter: LMS tells you what people learned, not whether it worked. LevelJump doesn't replace the LMS — it makes the LMS data meaningful.

**Ask**: typically aimed at revenue operations or enablement directors with budget authority.

### Key Lesson

The insight should be derived from your differentiated value, not invented independently. If you can't deliver what the insight promises, don't use it. If you can, it automatically disqualifies alternatives — because they can't say it either.

## 3. Postman

**Company**: API development platform, 20 million users, 500,000+ companies. Began as an API testing tool, expanded to cover the full API lifecycle: design, testing, documentation, distribution. Common buyer confusion: which tools does Postman replace? It spans multiple existing categories simultaneously.

**Positioning inputs**:
- Competitive alternatives: mix of siloed tools (each handles one lifecycle stage)
- Unique capabilities: integrated toolset spanning the full API lifecycle, collaboration workspaces, consumer-facing API repository
- Differentiated value: improved API quality, faster developer collaboration, higher API adoption
- Best-fit customers: engineering teams that build and distribute APIs as core business outputs
- Market category: API development platform (new category; previously no single label existed)

### Step 1 — Insight

**Insight used**: "APIs are becoming crucial building blocks of software. Low-quality, hard-to-consume APIs put customer experience and revenue at risk. Companies must quickly deliver high-quality, easily consumable APIs."

Why this insight works: it reframes the conversation from "what tools do you use for API work?" to "what is the business consequence of API quality?" Once the buyer accepts that API quality is a business risk, the fragmentation of their current toolset becomes the obvious source of that risk.

This is how to handle a product that crosses categories: don't try to explain which tool you replace. Instead, establish the business problem that makes replacement necessary.

### Step 2 — Alternatives

One approach cluster (not multiple):

**Siloed tools for each lifecycle stage** (separate tools for design, testing, documentation, distribution):
- Pros: each tool is best-in-class for its specific function, teams can choose independently
- Cons: (1) governance across the lifecycle is impossible — quality issues hide between stages, (2) APIs end up inconsistent and poorly documented because no single owner, (3) consumers can't easily discover or use the APIs because there's no unified portal

The single cluster works here because the pattern is the same regardless of which specific tools are used. The problem is the approach (siloed), not the brand.

### Step 3 — Perfect World

"An API-first approach: integrated tools spanning the lifecycle AND easy collaboration across teams AND a repository that gives consumers everything they need. Right?"

Three criteria map directly to the three failure points of siloed tools. Each criterion will become a value theme in the demo.

### Steps 4-5 — Introduction and Value Demo

Transition: "That's why we built Postman."

Demo organized by three value themes:

1. **Improves API quality via integrated toolset** — shows how design, testing, and documentation are connected; a change in design propagates through
2. **Workspaces for developer collaboration** — shows shared workspace, real-time collaboration, no more emailing Postman collections
3. **Improves adoption by making APIs easier to consume** — shows the public API repository (Postman Network), consumer documentation, one-click "Run in Postman"

### Steps 6-8 — Proof, Objections, Ask

**Proof**: adoption metrics (20M users, 500K companies) serve as social proof. Case studies show time-to-API-delivery reduction and measurable increase in API adoption rates.

**Objections**: "We already use [specific tool] for testing." Response: Postman integrates with or replaces that tool while adding the collaboration and distribution layers you don't have.

**Ask**: enterprise contracts often involve platform-wide adoption after team-level proof of concept.

### Key Lesson

When a product spans multiple categories, buyers get confused about which tool it replaces. The storyboard solves this by reframing from "replacement" to "why you need integration." The insight establishes the business stakes; the alternatives show that fragmentation is the root cause; the Perfect World names integration as the criterion. The buyer concludes that no single existing tool solves the problem — a platform is required.

## 4. Funnel

**Company**: Marketing data hub. Pulls data from any marketing data source (ad platforms, CRM, web analytics), normalizes it, and prepares it for analysis in any BI tool. Complex competitive landscape: compared to connectors, IT-managed data pipelines, and marketing analytics platforms depending on who evaluates it.

**Positioning inputs**:
- Competitive alternatives: connectors + spreadsheets; IT-managed data stacks; marketing analytics platforms
- Unique capabilities: marketing-owned, connects to any source, automatic normalization, outputs to any BI tool
- Differentiated value: marketers have a trusted, controlled, always-current single source of truth
- Best-fit customers: performance marketing teams at mid-to-large companies with complex multi-channel campaigns
- Market category: marketing data hub (category label Funnel helped create)

### Step 1 — Insight

**Insight used**: "Great digital marketing is all about data. But gathering data across channels, normalizing it, and producing metrics is difficult, time-consuming, and error-prone."

Why this insight works: every marketer agrees with the first sentence. The second sentence names the operational problem that prevents the first sentence from being true. The insight doesn't name a solution — it names a pain that every approach cluster fails to solve in different ways.

### Step 2 — Alternatives

Three approach clusters (most complex example):

**Connectors + spreadsheets** (Supermetrics-style tools + Excel):
- Pros: simple to set up, marketers can do it themselves, low cost
- Cons: data is siloed per channel, changes in source APIs break historical data, no normalization, Excel doesn't scale

**IT-centric data stack** (Fivetran + Snowflake + dbt + BI):
- Pros: robust, scalable, technically correct
- Cons: IT controls the pipeline, marketing gets static snapshots, can't trust or modify data without IT ticket, slow to change

**Marketing analytics platforms** (Adobe Analytics, similar):
- Pros: breadth of features, some automation
- Cons: locked into vendor's data model, requires IT for integration, requires technical expertise within marketing, still doesn't give marketers ownership

Three clusters are needed because Funnel is evaluated against all three depending on who is in the room. Each cluster fails at a different thing, but all fail at the same underlying requirement: marketing-owned, trusted, flexible data.

### Step 3 — Perfect World

"A central data hub that: pulls accurate data from any source AND stores it in a marketing-owned, trusted central place AND automatically prepares data for analysis AND makes it available wherever analysis happens. Right?"

Four criteria. Each maps to a demo theme. The "marketing-owned" phrase is critical — it disqualifies the IT-centric stack implicitly.

### Steps 4-5 — Introduction and Value Demo

Transition: "That's what Funnel is."

Demo organized by four value themes:

1. **Brings all data together from any source** — demo: connect a new data source in a few clicks; emphasize breadth of connectors
2. **Central hub marketing owns and trusts** — demo: the data hub interface; show version history and data lineage; highlight that no IT ticket is needed
3. **Auto-prepares data for analysis** — demo: automatic field mapping and normalization; historical data survives source changes
4. **Makes data available wherever needed** — demo: connect to a BI tool (Looker, Tableau); show the same data appearing in multiple outputs

Four themes matches four criteria exactly. The mapping is explicit.

### Steps 6-8 — Proof, Objections, Ask

**Proof**: case studies showing hours saved per week on data preparation, and reduction in "data trust" incidents (reports where numbers didn't match). Both metrics are operational, not vanity.

**Objections**: "We already have a BI tool." Response: Funnel feeds your BI tool better data. It's not a replacement — it's the layer underneath.

**Ask**: typically annual contract; evaluation involves marketing ops and sometimes IT sign-off.

### Key Lesson

When a product competes against multiple different categories depending on who is evaluating, the storyboard must acknowledge all clusters explicitly. Skipping one gives buyers an excuse to default back to it. Three clusters is more work, but it closes the door on each alternative before the demo begins. The "marketing data hub" label is itself a positioning innovation — it names a category that didn't exist, which allows Funnel to define the requirements for that category.

## 5. Gearset

**Company**: Salesforce DevOps platform, largest customer base in the category. Competitors exist and claim similar capabilities, but are architecturally different: most are built inside Salesforce (metadata-driven), while Gearset is built outside Salesforce (uses standard Git-based DevOps tooling). Key differentiators: uses standard DevOps tools (GitHub, Jira), 2,000+ deployments completed (4x nearest competitor), IBM and J&J as reference customers.

**Positioning inputs**:
- Competitive alternatives: limited; category is nascent, most companies are still doing manual Salesforce releases
- Unique capabilities: built outside Salesforce using standard tools, deepest deployment expertise
- Differentiated value: DevOps done the right way for Salesforce, not a Salesforce-specific workaround
- Best-fit customers: Salesforce development teams at enterprises that want to apply standard DevOps practices
- Market category: Salesforce DevOps

### Step 1 — Insight

**Insight used**: "DevOps is mainstream. The same factors that lead to DevOps success generally also apply to Salesforce releases. But not all DevOps transformations are equally effective."

Why this insight works: the first sentence is common knowledge — DevOps is established. The second sentence extends that knowledge to Salesforce specifically, which is not obvious (Salesforce has historically resisted standard DevOps tooling). The third sentence plants doubt: effectiveness varies. This sets up the Perfect World criteria (what makes a Salesforce DevOps transformation effective) without explicitly attacking competitors.

### Step 2 — Alternatives (Compressed)

The competitive landscape is limited: most prospects are either doing manual releases or just beginning to evaluate tools. There are not multiple well-established approach clusters with distinct trade-offs.

**Structural choice**: compress alternatives. Move from insight directly to Perfect World without building out detailed pros/cons tables. The storyboard does not require all steps to be equally weighted — when the landscape is thin, dwelling on alternatives creates confusion rather than clarity.

### Step 3 — Perfect World

"Successful Salesforce DevOps requires: (1) standard tools and processes, (2) deep DevOps expertise, (3) broad capabilities tailored to Salesforce's specific challenges. Right?"

Three criteria that are explicitly designed to match Gearset's specific advantages over competitors. Standard tools = Gearset's architectural choice. Deep expertise = deployment volume. Broad capabilities = feature depth.

Competitors who mirror Gearset's messaging cannot make these three criteria specific. They use the same words but cannot provide the same evidence.

### Steps 4-5 — Introduction and Value Demo

Transition: "That's exactly how we built Gearset."

Demo organized by three value themes:

1. **DevOps the right way** — standard tools integration: GitHub for source control, Jira for issue tracking, Asana for project management. Show that the workflow feels like standard DevOps, not a Salesforce-specific workaround.

2. **Industry's deepest understanding** — specific numbers: 2,000+ deployments, 4x nearest competitor. Named enterprise customers: IBM, Johnson & Johnson. These are not vague credibility claims — they are verifiable specifics that a competitor who says "we're also experts" cannot match.

3. **Broad and deep capabilities** — comparison tooling (shows what changed between environments), precision deployments (deploy exactly what you mean to deploy), release pipelines (multi-stage orchestration), backup and recovery. Each capability addresses a known failure mode of Salesforce releases.

### Steps 6-8 — Proof, Objections, Ask

**Proof**: deployment volume and named customers function as proof within the demo itself (theme 2). Additional case studies focus on specific deployment failures avoided and time saved in release cycles.

**Objections**: "Competitor X says they do the same thing." Counter: ask them for their deployment count and reference customers at this scale. Specificity is the weapon — a competitor who mirrors your claims cannot produce the same evidence.

**Ask**: enterprise contracts; evaluation typically involves Salesforce architects and DevOps leads.

### Key Lesson

Two structural lessons from Gearset:

**Lesson 1 — Compress when the landscape is thin.** Not every storyboard needs a full alternatives section with multiple clusters. When there are few credible alternatives, the compression is correct. Adding fake alternatives weakens the story.

**Lesson 2 — Specificity defeats mimicry.** When competitors use the same language as you, the answer is not different language — it is more specific evidence. Exact deployment counts, named customers at scale, and architectural specifics (built outside Salesforce using standard Git tooling) are claims that cannot be parroted. Where a competitor can only say "we're experts too," you say "2,000 deployments, 4x the nearest competitor, IBM and J&J."

## Cross-Case Pattern Summary

| Company | Insight Focus | Approach Clusters | Value Themes | Key Structural Choice |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|--------------|----------------------|
| Help Scout | Support as growth driver, not cost center | 2 | 3 | Alternatives eliminate both options before demo; conclusion is inevitable |
| LevelJump | Measure enablement with sales metrics, not completion | 2 | 3 | Insight derived directly from unique capability (CRM integration) |
| Postman | API quality is a business risk, fragmentation is the cause | 1 (siloed tools) | 3 | Reframes from "which tool do you replace?" to "why integration is required" |
| Funnel | Marketing data = competitive advantage; gathering it is broken | 3 | 4 | Three clusters needed because product is evaluated against three categories |
| Gearset | DevOps success factors apply to Salesforce | Compressed | 3 | Compressed alternatives + specificity as weapon against mirroring competitors |

### Structural rules extracted from these five cases

**Insight derivation**: derive the insight from your differentiated capability (LevelJump pattern). If you can't back up the insight with your product, don't use it. Insight should be something competitors would not say because they cannot deliver it.

**Cluster count**: match cluster count to the actual competitive landscape. Two clusters when there are two real alternatives (Help Scout, LevelJump). Three when prospects evaluate against three categories (Funnel). One when the problem is the approach rather than the brand (Postman). Compressed when the landscape is nascent (Gearset).

**Perfect World criteria count**: match criteria count to value theme count. Each criterion becomes a demo theme. If you have three criteria and four demo themes, the mapping breaks and buyers lose the thread.

**Compression trigger**: compress alternatives when the competitive landscape is thin or when the problem is the approach pattern rather than specific tools. Never invent alternatives to fill a structural slot.

**Specificity as differentiation**: when competitors mirror your positioning language, respond with specific evidence they cannot replicate — exact numbers, named enterprise customers, architectural specifics. Vague credibility claims are copyable; specific evidence is not.
