# Sales Pitch Storyboard: 8-Step Structure

Source: April Dunford, *Sales Pitch* (2023)

---

## Overview

The storyboard is a narrative framework for a sales pitch. It divides into two sequential phases:

- **Setup** (Steps 1–3): About the market and the prospect's context. Product is not mentioned.
- **Follow-Through** (Steps 4–8): About your solution. Begins only after prospect alignment is confirmed.

The transition between phases is gated by explicit prospect agreement at the end of Step 3. Never begin Follow-Through without passing the gate.

---

## Two-Phase Structure

| Phase | Steps | Focus | Trigger |
|-------|-------|-------|---------|
| Setup | 1–3 | Market context, alternatives, ideal criteria | Always first |
| Follow-Through | 4–8 | Solution, proof, objections, next step | Only after "Right?" confirmation |

---

## SETUP PHASE

### Step 1: Insight

**Purpose:** Establish your unique point of view on the market that makes your differentiated value relevant.

The Insight is not a product claim. It is a market observation — something true about the landscape that the prospect may not have fully articulated. It creates a frame that points toward the gap your product fills.

**Derivation:** The Insight is reverse-engineered from your Differentiated Value (see Positioning → Pitch Mapping below). Ask: "What must a prospect believe about the market for our differentiated value to matter?"

**Structure of a good Insight:**
- Grounded in a real market shift, dynamic, or constraint — not a generic observation every vendor could make
- Specific to the prospect's world (shaped by their role, industry, or stated problem)
- Points directionally toward gaps in existing approaches — without naming your product
- Delivered as a shared observation, not a vendor claim

**What makes an Insight weak:**
- States a problem every competitor also acknowledges (e.g., "Security is hard")
- Jumps to solution implication before establishing shared context
- Is so abstract it could apply to any product in the category

**Output of Step 1:** Prospect is oriented to a specific lens for evaluating their current situation. They are primed to think critically about existing alternatives.

---

### Step 2: Alternatives

**Purpose:** Map out the competitive landscape from the prospect's perspective — grouped by approach, with honest pros and cons.

This is conducted as a **discovery conversation**, not a monologue. The goal is to surface how the prospect currently handles the problem (or how they have evaluated options), and to surface the gaps in those approaches.

**Structure:**
1. Name 2–4 alternative approaches (not individual vendors — approaches or categories)
2. Acknowledge what each approach does well
3. Identify the limitation of each approach — particularly the gap your Insight predicts
4. Confirm with the prospect which alternatives they have considered or are using

**Critical constraint:** Do not position alternatives as simply "bad." Each must have a legitimate upside. The limitation must be genuine and traceable to the Insight established in Step 1. If the gap feels manufactured, the pitch loses credibility.

**Discovery framing:** "Have you looked at [approach]? What was your experience?" or "A lot of teams in your situation start with [approach] — does that match where you are?"

**Output of Step 2:** Shared understanding of the tradeoff landscape. Prospect has articulated (or confirmed) the limitations of current approaches. These limitations become the raw material for Step 3.

---

### Step 3: Perfect World

**Purpose:** Derive the criteria for an ideal solution from the Insight + the gaps identified in Step 2. End with explicit qualification gate.

**Structure:**
1. Synthesize: "Based on what you've described, it sounds like the ideal solution would need to..."
2. List 3–6 specific criteria — each one traceable to either (a) the Insight or (b) a gap in an existing alternative
3. End with: "Does that sound right?" or "Is that a fair summary of what you'd need?"

**Criteria quality standard:** Each criterion must be specific enough that a prospect could use it to eliminate at least one alternative from Step 2. Generic criteria (e.g., "easy to use," "good support") do not advance the pitch.

**The "Right?" Gate:**

This is the most structurally important moment in the storyboard.

| Prospect Response | Meaning | Action |
|-------------------|---------|--------|
| Yes, that's right | Agreed to criteria that map to your differentiated value. You have established the frame. | Proceed to Follow-Through (Step 4) |
| Partially / some caveats | One or more criteria missed or mismapped | Revisit specific criteria. Adjust Perfect World to reflect their actual priorities. Re-qualify before proceeding. |
| No / significant reframe | Prospect's actual criteria do not map to your differentiated value | This prospect is likely not a best-fit customer. Graceful disqualification is the correct outcome. Do not proceed to Follow-Through. |

**What graceful disqualification looks like:** "It sounds like [criterion X] is really the priority for you — that's actually not where we focus. We'd be wasting your time showing you [product]. Happy to recommend something that fits better."

**Output of Step 3:** Explicit prospect agreement that the criteria for an ideal solution match what your product delivers. This is the logical foundation for everything in Follow-Through.

---

## FOLLOW-THROUGH PHASE

Follow-Through begins only after "Right?" returns a yes or adjusted yes.

---

### Step 4: Introduction

**Purpose:** Name the market category. One sentence. Quick.

Example: "What I'm going to show you is [market category] — specifically built for [segment]."

This is not a product pitch paragraph. It is a single orienting sentence that places your product on a shelf in the prospect's mental model. It should take under 30 seconds.

**Common error:** Over-explaining the category or coining a new category name that requires further explanation. Use the category name the prospect already recognizes.

**Output of Step 4:** Prospect knows what type of thing they are about to see.

---

### Step 5: Differentiated Value Demo

**Purpose:** Demonstrate how your product delivers the differentiated value that satisfies the Perfect World criteria. This is the bulk of the pitch.

**Organizing principle:** Structure the demo around **value themes**, not feature walkthrough order. Maximum 3 value themes. Each theme maps directly to one or more criteria from Step 3.

**What a value theme is:** A cluster of features + outcomes that together deliver one specific dimension of differentiated value. Example: "Automated compliance tracking" is a theme. The features within it (audit log, role-based access, exportable reports) are sub-components.

**Three delivery formats:**

| Format | When to use |
|--------|------------|
| Slides + demo | Complex product, prospect needs context before seeing the UI |
| Demo only | Product is self-explanatory; slides slow things down |
| Slides only | No product to show yet (pre-launch, pilot), or remote async delivery |

**Within each value theme:**
1. Restate the criterion from Step 3 that this theme addresses
2. Show (or describe) how the product delivers it
3. Connect outcome to the gap identified in Step 2 alternatives

**What to exclude:** Features that do not map to a value theme derived from Step 3 criteria. Every feature shown must answer "why does this matter given what they agreed to in Step 3?"

**Output of Step 5:** Prospect has seen evidence that your product delivers the criteria they agreed to. The logical case is made; Steps 6–8 close and confirm.

---

### Step 6: Proof

**Purpose:** Back up the value claims made in Step 5 with external validation.

Use the highest tier of proof available for each claim. Do not present all tiers — select the best-fit evidence for each value theme.

**8-Tier Proof Hierarchy (strongest to weakest):**

| Tier | Type | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| 1 | Matching case study | Same segment, same problem, same outcome. Most persuasive. |
| 2 | Third-party verification | Independent audit, lab test, or certification body result |
| 3 | Certifications | Industry or regulatory certification (SOC 2, ISO, etc.) |
| 4 | Customer-validated statistics | Metrics from real customers, verified and citable |
| 5 | Customer quotes | Named, with title and company. Specific outcome claim preferred. |
| 6 | Analyst quotes / review platform ratings | Gartner, G2, Forrester, etc. |
| 7 | Third-party research | Industry report or academic study that supports your claims |
| 8 | Awards | Lowest tier. Useful only when nothing else is available. |

**Matching case study requirements (Tier 1):** Same industry or segment as the prospect, same problem described in Setup, quantified outcome. If you have this, lead with it. If you don't have an exact match, go down the hierarchy.

**Output of Step 6:** Prospect's belief in the value claims is reinforced by external evidence. Risk of "sounds good but is it real?" is reduced.

---

### Step 7: Objections (Optional)

**Purpose:** Proactively surface and address unspoken concerns that would prevent the deal from advancing.

This step is **conditional**: only include it when (a) you know a common objection exists and (b) you have a good answer. Do not raise objections you cannot fully resolve — this creates doubt without relief.

**When to use proactive objection handling:**
- Prospect segment reliably raises the same concern (e.g., "how long does implementation take?")
- A known competitor FUD exists in the market
- A surface-level weakness of your product is likely to be noticed

**Format:** State the concern directly, then resolve it. Do not hedge.

Example: "One thing teams often ask at this point is [concern]. Here's how that actually works: [resolution]."

**What this is not:** A Q&A. Proactive objection handling is scripted for predictable concerns. Unpredictable questions are handled in actual Q&A after the formal storyboard.

**Output of Step 7:** Known deal-blockers are neutralized before they become silent reasons for inaction.

---

### Step 8: The Ask

**Purpose:** Recommend the next step. Do not present options or ask the prospect what they want to do.

The ask is a specific, confident recommendation: "The next step I'd suggest is [X]. Does [date/time] work?"

**What to ask for (examples by stage):**
- Pilot or proof-of-concept kickoff
- Technical evaluation meeting
- Proposal or pricing discussion
- Procurement call
- Contract review

**What not to do:** "So... what are your thoughts?" or "What would you like to do next?" These leave the prospect in control of a process they have less context about than you do. You know what the next logical step is. Recommend it.

**Output of Step 8:** A concrete next step is agreed and scheduled, or the reason for not proceeding is explicit.

## Positioning → Pitch Mapping

The 5 positioning components from *Obviously Awesome* map directly to storyboard steps:

| Positioning Component | Storyboard Step(s) |
|-----------------------|--------------------|
| Competitive Alternatives | Step 2 (Alternatives) |
| Unique Capabilities | Step 5 (feeds into value theme demo structure) |
| Differentiated Value | Step 1 (Insight is derived from it), Step 3 (Perfect World criteria map to it), Step 5 (Value Demo demonstrates it) |
| Best-Fit Customers | Step 1 (Insight is shaped by their perspective), informs champion targeting |
| Market Category | Step 4 (Introduction) |

Steps 6–8 (Proof, Objections, Ask) are not derived from positioning. They come from sales process knowledge: what evidence is available, what objections recur, what the correct next step is.

**Implication:** A team with no positioning work cannot build a differentiated storyboard. The Insight in Step 1 will be generic. The Perfect World in Step 3 will match criteria any competitor could also claim. Positioning must precede pitch construction.

## Timing Guidelines

| Call Length | Setup (Steps 1–3) | Value Demo (Step 5) | Proof + Objections + Ask (Steps 6–8) |
|-------------|-------------------|----------------------|---------------------------------------|
| 30 minutes | 2–3 minutes | 20–22 minutes | 5–8 minutes |
| 60 minutes | 5–10 minutes | 30–40 minutes | 10–15 minutes |
| 90 minutes | 5–10 minutes | 50–60 minutes | 15–20 minutes |

**Setup minimum:** Setup can be compressed to 90 seconds if the prospect has already seen prior content (webinar, outbound sequence, previous call). The gate ("Right?") still must be passed.

**Never skip Setup entirely:** Without Steps 1–3, the demo in Step 5 has no frame. The prospect evaluates features in isolation, not against criteria they agreed to. This produces "looks interesting, let me think about it" — which is a stall.

**Value Demo is the pitch:** Steps 1–3, 4, 6, 7, 8 are structural scaffolding. Step 5 is where the deal is won or lost. Time allocation should reflect this.

## What This Structure Replaces

Four common pitch formats and why they are insufficient for a differentiated product:

| Format | Core Problem |
|--------|-------------|
| **Product Walkthrough** | Presents features without value context. No space for market discussion. Prospect evaluates completeness, not differentiated fit. |
| **Problem/Solution** | States a generic problem that every competitor in the category also claims to solve. Differentiation never surfaces. |
| **Vision Narrative** | Designed for investors (why the future will look different). Gives customers a reason to wait rather than act. |
| **Hero's Journey** | No rubric for prospect to evaluate alternatives. Emotional framing without logical criteria. Cannot generate the qualifying gate. |

The storyboard replaces all four by establishing shared criteria (Perfect World) before revealing the solution, then demonstrating fit against those criteria in the Value Demo.

## Build Order and Team Roles

Build sequence:
1. Storyboard first (narrative flow, no slides)
2. Value themes and demo script
3. Slides and visual assets
4. Proof assets (case study selection, quote curation)
5. Rehearsal and timing calibration

Team roles:

| Role | Contribution |
|------|-------------|
| Founder / CEO | Validates Insight (market POV), approves Perfect World criteria |
| Product | Identifies which capabilities map to which value themes |
| Marketing | Writes slides, wordsmithing, proof asset curation |
| Sales | Validates timing, flow, objection list, The Ask wording |
| Customer Success | Supplies case studies, customer quotes, outcome data for Proof |

Building the storyboard without sales input produces a pitch with wrong timing. Building it without product input produces value themes that don't match what the product actually does. All roles participate in the storyboard build; marketing and design execute the final assets.
